Luggage Handling

For all those of you who take the plane and feel that it's a time consuming and boring process (especially when flying frequently) there is a whole world which you rarely see.

As a passenger you find it can be quite a struggle dealing with your 20 kilograms of luggage and skies when coming over to Geneva for your winter holiday. At the point when you leave your skies and go for a coffee or tea the skies are entering into the world of luggage handling.

Container luggage

This world is a complex world of destinations, transfers, containers, chariots, lifts, ground crew, trains and more.

As the baggage is identified and enters the tri baggage (luggage sorting) every single suitcase is passed through a system of platforms and transported to a slide down which it goes waiting to be put into a container. There are several containers around a team of luggage handlers and they read the label seeing whether it is short stay, transfer, direct, first class and business, standby or train. If they are standby and train it's simple, they sit there and the barcode is checked to make sure that the client/passenger has checked in. Once the passenger is checked in they are transferred to the appropriate location. With normal luggage it goes into one container, business into another and first class into a third.

Free luggage

Free luggage as I will refer to it here is luggage for flights and aircrafts where containers may not be used for the items. In this case each piece of luggage is added to a chariot, one side where the cases are vertical and the other where they are horizontal. Each of these chariots will hold around 2-300 kilos and you may find from 10 to 15 chariots for just one aircraft depending on the wheight of the luggage and how efficiently it is packed. In general this process can last two hours, from the time the check in opens to the time you and your luggage are boarding the plane.

Aircraft luggage handling

Unloading:

Unloading aircrafts is a very good physical work out. Depending on the plane you may be one or two people in the hold where you get very warm from the work. You have a wall of luggage piled at least a meter high and several meters deep. As you begin the work it's quite easy with the light bags higher in the stack and the heavier near the bottom. This part of luggage handling is quite similar to a giant game of tetris (although far heavier). Suitcases are usually at the bottom as they create a base from which to pile the other luggage. Each piece of luggage is placed parralel to the aircraft's body. It's several layers thick and as you remove one wall you get to the next one and remove that one until finally you reach the end and the aircraft is empty. In certain aircrafts you find belly loading systems such as conveyor belts or trays from which you remove the luggage.

Loading

After the effort of unloading a plane we are now greeted by the fun or reloading the plane with the outgoing luggage and this can be quite a tiring effort as you are kneeling to lift bags which can be quite heavy. Depending on the aircraft you will find yuo need to put the bags several meters deep into the plane and start building piles of luggage. This is the most tiring and back breaking part of aircraft loading because you have time constraints within which to create your three dimensional tetris wall. From personal experience each chariot equals to around one layer in the hold. Lifting the luggage in such space takes a lot of strength and it's impossible to load a whole plane without using leverage as far as possible. When suitcases need to be put higher up the knee is quite good as a lever for lifting the suitcase and placing it within the available space. When loading skies you will usually find that the skies and snowboards are put either at the tail part of the plane or at the front depending on the aircraft. They are piled up quite high and are treated seperately from the baggage.

Charter Luggage arrivals

I worked at the charter arrivals at Geneva airport during around three hours one day and the amount of planes landing and departing was quite impressive. As each plane is unloaded by the ground crew by the aircraft the chariots are then brought to the terminal building where they are passed from the chariot towards the conveyor belt which takes the luggage to the clients waiting on the other side of the wall. During this phase although it is quite intensive it is not that hard physically, consisting mainly in taking luggage and putting it down, in most cases just levering from chariot to conveyor efficiently to keep the passengers happy.

Skies are usually pulled into the room where the people have retrieved their luggage and it's quite amusing to see how they rush towards their skies, try to get their skies and leave the rest on the floor for others to get theirs.

Conclusion

I enjoy working as a luggage handler although the work is very physically demanding. It is a team of international people who joke and get tired together whilst making sure you arrive and leave on time. When you see luggage handlers lying on a chariot or sitting appearing to do nothing remember that they may have just treated up to four tons of luggage in the last half hr or so. They/we are not lazy and you'll see this from the muscle mass gained by these workers, of which in winter, many are students doing the job for a little extra cash to make life a little more comfortable.
